\chapter{Conclusion and Perspective for Future Work} \label{conc}

The infiltration of pests into commodities is an inevitable risk throughout the supply chain. Even though efforts can be made during cultivation, harvesting, and storage to minimize the risk of infestation, this requires active monitoring and mitigation efforts. Beyond the direct loss of product from pest consumption, their presence can degrade the quality of the commodity, spread disease and pathogen, and cause financial losses. Trade of the infested product can lead to the spread of pests to new regions, introducing invasive species and result in further economic and ecological damage. 

Various methods have been developed to detect the presence of insects in stored products, leveraging physical devices to separate insects from the commodity, chemical sensors to measure insect-produced compounds in the material, and apertures to capture the visual signatures of the insects across the electromagnetic spectrum. Acoustic methods use the sounds generated by the insects in the product to identify and estimate the level of infestation. 

The Acoustic Stored Product Insect Detection System (A-SPIDS) was developed as a low-cost, portable, rapid, and non-destructive alternative inspection tool to the current standard of visual inspection and manual sampling at United States Ports of Entry. 

The A-SPIDS platform was used to capture the acoustic impulses generated by the live inspect specimens of the cowpea beetle, \textit{Callosobrucus maculatus}, the confused flour beetle, \textit{Tribolium confusum}, and adult and larval stages of the \textit{Tenebrio molitor}, known as the darkling beetle and yellow mealworm, respectively, when placed in stored product materials including oatmeal, rice, flour, wheat groats, and Corn Flakes. The integrated piezoelectric sensors lining the bottom of an enclosure capable of sampling up to \SI{1.5}{\liter} of material were sufficient to identify the sounds generated by a single insect occupying the material and the distributed array design allowed for the localization of the insect within the platform.

In order to address the different sensitives between the piezoelectric sensors, a normalization method was derived using the self-noise of the sensors to scale the recorded signals. A secondary normalization algorithm using the median of the recorded signal was also derived to support recordings, such as those in publicly available datasets, where the self-noise of the sensors is not available. These normalization methods guided the  formation of the Normalized Signal Pulse Amplitude (NSPA) and the Normalized Signal Energy Level (NSEL) features, parameters which characterized the recorded signals using their amplitude and spectral components, respectively. 

Since acoustic sensors are susceptible to the influence of external ambient noise, the A-SPIDS platform was exposed to various noise sources at different levels of intensity. The system box-in-a-box design featuring silicon and acoustic foam insulation was shown to provide attenuation at a maximum of almost \SI{59}{\deci\bel} and an average of \SI{45}{\deci\bel} above \SI{2000}{\hertz} but was not sufficient to mitigate against strong impulsive external noises that generated insect-like signals. 

Spectral examination of the insect signals against external noise revealed a critical distinction. While external noise exhibited dominant spectral power below \SI{1600}{\hertz}, insect-generated vibrations demonstrated higher energy content above this frequency. By optimizing the frequency band for the NSPA detection metric, \SI{99.4}{\percent} of the insect-material pairings in the dataset subset exceeded the maximum NSPA value generated by external noise at \SI{80}{\deci\bel A}. 

Integration of the external microphone in the developed algorithm enabled the identification and removal of noise-generated impulses from the internal piezoelectric sensor recordings by exploiting the penetration coefficient relationship between the external and internal sensors. The improved system achieved \SI{100}{\percent} insect detection with zero false positives across all tested conditions, including external noise levels up to \SI{100}{\deci\bel A}.

The recordings made by the A-SPIDS platform were collected and published as the Stored Product Insect Dataset (SPID), available on Kaggle \cite{danielkadyrovStoredProductInsecta}. A Python package, the Stored Product Insect Database (SPIDB), was developed to support the loading, processing, visualization, and analysis of the data \cite{danielkadyrovDkadyrovSpidbSPIDB2024}. Examples of its usage are provided in its documentation as well as the repositories for earlier publications \cite{danielkadyrovDkadyrovSPIDAcousticV100a2026}. The programming scripts used in this dissertation are available in the appendix and associated repository. The dataset and package provide a resource for the research community to further explore the acoustic signatures of stored product insects and develop new methods for their detection. 

The presented A-SPIDS prototype was tested in laboratory conditions but requires further investigation of its performance in field conditions within food-storage facilities and at Ports of Entry. Field deployment at operational inspection sites could provide critical data to refine the system design and algorithms based on real-world performance. Such installation would yield insights into the variability of ambient noise profiles, substrate types and conditions, insect behaviors encountered during routine inspection, and user feedback on the practical aspects of the system design and operation. 

The current A-SPIDS model can guide the development of future iterations of the system. The high sensitivity and SNR of the platform demonstrated its potential to handle high volumes of material. Additional sensors could be added to the platform such as accelerometers to control external vibration and visual sensors to assist in the identification of the insect species. Features could be integrated into the algorithm to further enhance the detection accuracy and noise resilience. Implementation of machine learning approaches trained on the SPID dataset could provide more sophisticated detection and classification capabilities as well as adaptability to diverse noise conditions.
