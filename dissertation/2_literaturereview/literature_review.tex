\section{Stored Food Product}

Stored product refers to nonperishable raw and processed commoditized food items that can be stored for extended periods. These include grains, legumes, nuts, spices, sugar, dried fruits, and processed food \cite{neethirajanDetectionTechniquesStoredProduct2007}.

Grains include cereals, pulses, and oilseeds. Cereals are grasses cultivated for its edible grain such as wheat, rice, corn, rye, millet, and barley \cite{awikaMajorCerealGrains2011}. Aptly categorized, these grains are the most commonly produced, consumed, and traded food commodities worldwide. Global cereal supply totalled a record high of 3029 million tonnes in 2025 \cite{FAOCerealSupply}. Wheat, rice, maize, and soybeans provide two-thirds of the world's caloric intake \cite{zhaoTemperatureIncreaseReduces2017}.

Pulses are the edible seeds of the \textit{Leguminoseae} family and include beans, lentils, peas, and chickpeas. Peanuts, soybeans, and alfalfa are legumes but are considered to be oilseeds, such as sunflower seeds, rapeseeds, and sesame seeds \cite{sathiyaseelanMajorInsectPests2022}. The worldwide oil seed production was projected to be 695.78 million tonnes for the year 2025/2026 \cite{WorldAgriculturalSupply2025}. Stored products also include nuts, spices, sugar, dried fruits, and processed food \cite{neethirajanDetectionTechniquesStoredProduct2007}. Despite global events at the time, grain and oilseed trade volumes reached 880 million tonnes in 2024 with a valuation of \$330 billion USD \cite{GrainsOilseedsWorld}.

Stored food products are susceptible to a variety of pests that can infest the material at any point from the field to storage. These pests include insects, mites, rodents, birds, and microorganisms such as bacteria, fungi, and molds \cite{devipriyar.InsectPestDetection2022}. The research and methods discussed in this review and thesis focus on insect pests, which represent a significant threat to stored products and high difficulty in detection.

\section{Stored Product Insect Pests}

Stored product insects (SPPs) are represented by more than 600 species of Coleopterans, 70 species of lepidopterans, and about 355 species of mites \cite{tyagiPostHarvestStoredProduct2019}.

Insects attack stored products in a variety of ways and are categorized by their feeding behavior. The rice weevil, \textit{Sitophilus oryzae}, the granary weevil, \textit{Sitophilus granarius}, and the lesser grain borer, \textit{Rhyzopertha dominica}, are examples of internal feeders, which bore into the kernels of grains and seeds as larvae and feed on the endosperm, visually undetectable, until they emerge as adults leaving burrowing holes and powder residue. External feeders, such as the drugstore beetle, \textit{Stegobium paniceum}, the cigarette beetle, \textit{Lasioderma serricorne}, and the khapra beetle, \textit{Trogoderma granarium}, chew through the outer seed coat of the grain exterior and then devour the inside. The red flour beetle, \textit{Tribolium castaneum}, and the saw-toothed grain beetle, \textit{Oryzaephilus surinamensis} are examples of secondary pests and scavengers feeders, which prefer broken down and processed material such as split kernels and flour \cite{bennettTrumansScientificGuide1988}.

Stored product pests (SPPs) can infest crops at any point from the field to storage. They can settle in unharvested crops and undetectably hitchhike on the harvested product to infiltrate storage facilities with favorable conditions for their proliferation. While storage facilities might introduce control mechanisms, major sources of infestation are old bags, storage structures, and equipment \cite{tyagiPostHarvestStoredProduct2019}. Even elevator boots, enclosed spaces at the bottom of grain elevators, can accumulate residual grain and are seldom cleaned, enabling an ideal habitat for unnoticed infestations \cite{tilleyStoredgrainInsectPopulation2014}. As the product is packaged, transported, and distributed, the insects can cross-contaminate other products and spread the infestation to new locations.

Infestations lead to both quantitative and qualitative losses. Direct consumption of grain reduces its weight and volume, while contamination from excrement, webbing, and carcasses degrades its quality. Damage to the grain embryos significantly reduces the vital nutrients such as sugar, proteins, lipids, minerals, and vitamins within the product. In high fat grains, insect consumption can increase the breakdown of fat into fatty acids. Conversely, while decreasing the nutritional quality, the level of dietary fiber is increased which provides less effective absorption of nutrients from the food during human digestion and aggravating malnutrition \cite{bamaiyiEffectCallosobruchusMaculatus2006}. Insect blight also promotes infections, attracts other pathogens, and reduces seed viability. Furthermore, SPPs can damage storage containers and equipment \cite{devipriyar.InsectPestDetection2022}. The rate of infestation is accelerated by high temperatures, and the moisture and heat generated by the inhabitants create a more favorable environment for their proliferation \cite{muller-blenkleMethodAcousticStorage2023}.

An unmitigated infestation can lead to significant ecological, agricultural, and economic impact. Estimates by the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) of the United Nations suggest that \SI{20}{\percent} of the global food production is lost to herbivorous insects \cite{mohamedn.sallamInsectDamagePostHarvest2013}. Other sources state that post-harvest insects are responsible for \SIrange{10}{20}{\percent} of storage losses \cite{phillipsBiorationalApproachesManaging2010,gogiToxicityRepellencePlant2016} and cost up to \$470 billion USD per year in 2001 \cite{aliSustainableManagementInsect2023}. Globally, stored product pests are the primary driver of 1.3 billion tons of lost commodities, inflicting over \$100 billion USD annually \cite{harmanIncreasingGlobalRisk2025}. In India, insects represent \SI{26}{\percent} of the \SIrange{10}{30}{\percent} crop loss experienced annually \cite{tyagiPostHarvestStoredProduct2019}. Annual post-harvest losses to insects in Sub-Saharan Africa, where food insecurity is a significant issue, are estimated to be between \SIrange{30}{50}{\percent} \cite{mawonekeFarmForkReview2025}.

Introduction of non-native insects into ecological environments can further exacerbate negative effects if the species has no natural predators. Invasive species cost the United States \$2 trillion USD from 1960 to 2020, costing \$1 billion USD a year from 2010 to 2020 in damages, losses, and prevention \cite{fantle-lepczykEconomicCostsBiological2022}. Global economic costs are estimated to at least \$70 billion USD annually through direct crop destruction and management expenditures \cite{bradshawMassiveGrosslyUnderestimated2016}.

The khapra beetle, \textit{Trogoderma granarium}, also known as the cabinet beetle, represents one of the most significant stored product threats. It is capable of destroying \SIrange{30}{70}{\percent} of an infested commodity. Its larvae can survive years without food through diapause, are highly resistant to insecticides, prefer hiding in cracks and crevices of storage containers, and produce an abundance of exuviae and frass during their infestation. Native to South Asia, it has not become established in the United States. The United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) estimates that an established infestation would result in economic consequences costing up to billions of dollars from direct crop losses and immediate embargoes from trading partners. In the 1950s, the US spent 13 years eradicating infestations at 600 sites across southwestern states with a cost of \$141.8 million USD \cite{harmanIncreasingGlobalRisk2025}. As a result, the US maintains a zero-tolerance policy and enforces strict phytosanitary restrictions and inspections for products that might contain the khapra beetle \cite{u.s.departmentofagricultureanimalandplanthealthinspectionserviceKhapraBeetleProgram2022}. Recently, CBP agricultural specialists found \textit{T. granarium} remains during a secondary inspection of an undeclared seeds \cite{RemainsWorldsMost2026}.

Other impactful insect species include the larger grain borer, \textit{Rhyzopertha dominica}, which costs Sub-Saharan Africa an estimated \$4 billion USD annually \cite{zoryaMissingFoodCase2011} and the rice weevil, \textit{Sitophilus oryzae}, which contributes to an annual storage loss of over \$1.5 billion USD \cite{banerjiReviewArticleBiology2025, chekolInfluenceRiceVariety2025}. United States rice farmers have been facing significant losses, up to \SI{75}{\percent}, to the rice delphacid, \textit{Tagosodes orizicolus}. The insect, native to Central and South America, damages the rice plant by sucking out the sap and transmits the hoja balnca virus further damaging yield or killing the plant completely. It is not clear if the insect established itself by natural means or through human-mediated transport \cite{marksTinyPestsAre}.

There are several methods to control insect infestations in stored products including cultural, physical, chemical, microbial, biotechnical, and botanical control \cite{katooCallosobruchusMaculatusBiotic2025}. Although pesticides and fumigants are commonly used to control insect infestations, these chemicals may be dangerous to human, animal, and agroecosystem health. Relying on selected insecticides is not a solution against diverse insect species, some of which can develop resistance or already have complete tolerance to the chemicals \cite{akbarEvaluationInsecticidalPotentials2022,akbarEvaluatingEfficacyPlant2024}. Despite these methods, inspection throughout the supply chain is crucial to mitigate the spread of infestation and prevent the introduction of invasive species.

\section{Inspection Methods for Stored Product Insect Infestation}

\subsection{Standards for Inspecting Stored Product for Pests}

The International Organization of Standards (ISO) standard 6639 guides the means of storing cereals and pulses as well as controlling and detecting attacks by pests. It specifies that a container is considered infested if an insect is found within \SI{1}{\kilo\gram} of the material sample. During inspection, the collected material sample is divided into \SIrange{200}{300}{\gram} test samples. If insects are not found, the material is considered to be free of infestation \cite{ISO6639Cereals1986}. The USDA also guides the Customs and Border Protection Agriculture Specialists (CBPAS) to deem a container infested if one or two insects are found within \SI{1}{\kilo\gram} of material sample \cite{service2015a}.

\subsection{Methods for Inspecting Stored Products for Insects}

The main method employed at United States Ports of Entry to inspect stored product for insects is through manual inspection, where the chosen commodity is emptied on an inspection table and personnel, often with tools including sieves, tweezers, magnifying glasses, and intense lights, sift through the material looking for live and dead insects at different life stages, fragments, or signs of insect infestation such as webbing, casings, frass, or burrowing holes in the kernels. This process is time and physically consuming, requiring experienced personnel or with a background in entomology to identify the species of the insect in order to determine the appropriate course of action. If the insect is identified as a quarantine pest, the material is either treated, destroyed, or rejected and an Emergency Action Notification (EAN) is issued to the importer \cite{service2015a}.

The ISO 6639 standard describes several methods for detecting insects in stored grain products, focusing on carbon dioxide analysis, the ninhydrin method, the whole grain flotation method, as well as acoustic and X-ray methods \cite{ISO6639Cereals1986}. Various tools and devices have been developed, categorized by the sensing technologies they leverage, in order to expedite areas within the inspection including automating the examination process, detecting insect infestation, or identifying the insect species. The application of these inspection methods has to balance the accuracy of detecting and classifying pests of various life stages with safeguarding product quality, minimizing associated costs, ensuring the efficiency of supply chain movement, and limiting product damage and loss during examination.

If the inspection process involves commodities packaged in sealed containers, a single package can be opened and examined. If the commodity is stored in bulk, a sample must be extracted from the mass. Grain triers are able to take samples stored in bins often transported by trucks and railcars. Pelican samplers are long, thin tubes with a pointed end that can be inserted into the grain as it is being moved in a grain stream or loaded and unloaded, often automated to perform at regular intervals \cite{serviceStoredProductManagement1995}.

Ownership of the sample is important to consider as it can be a source of conflict between the importer and the inspector. Destructive inspection methods involve decimating the sample, which can lead to loss of product and revenue for the importer. Non-destructive methods, on the other hand, allow for the inspection of the sample without causing damage, preserving its value and integrity.

\subsubsection{Physical Methods}

Physical inspection and detection methods rely on the direct capture and enumeration of insects through traps, sieves, and other mechanical means.
%  Although they leverage primative technology, these methods are often low-cost and have been effectively scaled to process large volumes of product. 

\textit{Trapping Mechanisms}

The earliest form of stored product insect detection is through physical detection, specifically the Berlese-Tullgren trap. The biologist Antonio Berlese presented his eponymous funnel in 1905 where a sample of material was suspended on a screen mesh in a funnel. As the material is heated, in the original design through heated water, the insects move downwards to escape the heat and are collected in a container, filled with water or alcohol, the bottom of the funnel \cite{berleseApparecchioRaccoglierePresto1905}. The design was updated by Albert Tullgren in 1918 by introducing an electric light bulb as the heat source \cite{prof.alb.tullgrenSehrEinfacherAusleseapparat1918}. Variations on the design are common, low-cost, and easily implemented, but it requires a minimum of \SI{6}{\hour} to drive out live, mobile adult and larval insects from \SI{1}{\kilo\gram} sample \cite{robinwilkinComparisonTwoApproaches1996}. By using more powerful heat sources, decreasing the sample depth, and multiple sample trays, were shown to effectively extract the rusty flat grain beetle, \textit{Cryptolestes ferrugineus}, from \SI{1}{\kilo\gram} wheat samples in under \SI{1}{\hour} \cite{minkevichRapidMethodDetect2002}.

Probe traps, also known as pitfalls, are cylindrical tubes with perforations in the upper sections through which insects drop into a collection chamber. They feature pointed tips which allows them to be inserted at different depths of the storage container for extensive amounts of time and periodically removed to determine the number, species, and density of the infestation. This method is restricted to easily accessible locations within the container, limits the temporal availability of the results, and the capture rate is influenced by the insect species, the grain type, as well as environmental conditions including temperature and humidity \cite{neethirajanDetectionTechniquesStoredProduct2007}. To increase the capture rate, visual lures and pheromones could be used to attract insects into the traps.

Light trapping takes advantage of insect phototaxis, locomotion movement towards or away from a light stimulus, to direct insects towards probes and traps. Red and yellow colored light-emitting diodes (LEDs) demonstrated the most effective trapping performance against the maize weevil, \textit{Sitophilus zeamais}, \textit{T. castaneum}, and the \textit{R. dominica}. The \textit{R. dominica} was especially sensitive to light sources with wavelengths of \SI{373}{\nano\meter} \cite{gongTechnologiesSystemsPest2026}. Although safe, highly efficient, non-destructive, low-cost, and low risk of insect resistance, light trapping is limited to phototactic insect species, time of deployment, and is influenced by the intensity, wavelength, and duration of the light source as well the sampling container \cite{stermerSpectralResponseCertain1959, gongTechnologiesSystemsPest2026}.

Similarly, insects can exhibit attraction to different colors which can be used to coat traps to increase the capture rate. The confused flour beetle, \textit{Tribolium confusum}, \textit{T. castaneum}, \textit{S. oryzae}, and \textit{S. zeamais} showed different color preferences, with red, orange, and black most notably attracting the \textit{T. castaneum} and \textit{S. zeamais} \cite{gongTechnologiesSystemsPest2026}.

Insects use pheromones, released chemical substances, as communication mechanisms for mating, aggregation, and foraging behaviors. Artificial replicates can be set up to direct insects towards traps. Aggregation pheromones, 4,8-dimethyldecanal and dominicalure-1/2 effectively attracted \textit{T. castaneum} and \textit{R. dominica}. Sexual pheromones effectively lured the Indianmeal moth, \textit{Plodia interpunctella} \cite{gongTechnologiesSystemsPest2026}.

The Electronic Grain Probe Insect Counter (EGPIC) automates the process of counting insects captured by a probe trap inserted into the grain. The probe consists of a series of electrodes that create an electric field. As insects pass through the probe, they disrupt the field, generating electrical signals that are detected and counted by the system \cite{litzkowElectronicGrainProbe1997, shumanCommercializationElectronicGrain2001}.

While probes remain in the material for a set time, gravity and compartmented spears, also known as grain triers, can select \SIrange{0.2}{5}{\kilo\gram} of sample to be examined at a time \cite{wilkinDetectionInsectsGrain1990,serviceStoredProductManagement1995}.

\textit{Sieving Mechanisms}

Sieves utilize the size difference between the insect and the grain to separate them through a mesh with specific pore sizes. Although human operated sieves are a common tool already applied during manual inspection, sieves have been automated to process larger volumes of product at higher speeds.

An aspirated sieve, which consisted of a de-awner, an aspiration chamber, and a sequence of inclined, reciprocating sieves with different mesh sizes was able to remove \SI{90}{\percent} of \textit{O. surinamensis}, \textit{S. granarius}, and \textit{C. ferrugineus} from \SI{1}{\kilo\gram} samples with only \SI{4.5}{\percent} weight loss. Despite this high removal rate, the populations of the insects partially recovered \cite{armitageUseAspiratedSieve1996}.

The Insectomat 5K is an example of a rolling sieve that can screen up to \SI{10}{\kilo\gram} of exclusively wheat and barley sample in under \SI{2}{minutes} with an accuracy of 0.1 insects per \si{\kilo\gram} and an advertised \SI{100}{\percent} detection rate \cite{Insectomat, robinwilkinComparisonTwoApproaches1996}.

Although the systems are able to process large volumes of product quickly, they are fairly expensive, and require specialized calibration for the specific grain type and kernel size.

\textit{Electrical Conductance}

Electrical conductance machines monitor for changes in electrical conductance between whole, damaged, and infested kernels.

The Perten Single Kernel Characterization System (SKCS) monitors compression force and electrical conductance as individual kernels are crushed. A kernel acts as a single resistor in a two-resistor and voltage-divider circuit where differences in the conductance as the voltage is applied can characterize and differentiate the kernels. A low voltage measurement corresponds to low kernel resistance, typical of high moisture content kernels. A downward slope in the conductance curve can be an indication of a live insect present inside the kernel. Although it is more commonly used for wheat hardness determination, this method was able to identify up to \SI{88}{\percent} of larvae and pupae, from the \textit{S. oryzae} and \textit{R. dominica}, internally feeding in wheat kernels without misclassifying uninfested kernels. They were also able to detect kernels with dead insects up to \SI{72}{\percent} \cite{t.c.pearsonAUTOMATEDDETECTIONINTERNAL2003, neethirajanDetectionTechniquesStoredProduct2007}.

The electrical conductance method was automated by using a roller mill that was able to process \SI{1}{\kilo\gram} of crushed whole wheat in \SI{1}{\minute}. It was able to detect grains infested with \textit{Rhyzopertha dominica} with a detection rate of up to three insects per \SIrange{1}{2}{\kilo\gram} \cite{t.pearsonDetectionWheatKernels2007, brabecDetectionInternalInsects2010}.

Similar to the sieving method, the electrical conductance method requires specialized calibration for the specific grain type and kernel size.

\textit{Flotation Method}

The flotation method uses density differences between infested, damaged, and whole kernels when submerged in a liquid. Fiber rich substrates are hygroscopic and increase in weight through water absorption while insects are hydrophobic due to the high lipid content \cite{germinaraDevelopmentFlotationMethod2010}. This method destroys the sample and underestimates the level of infestation but provides rapid qualitative results to detect and classify the infestation \cite{ISO6639Cereals1986}.

The whole grain flotation method is described within the ISO 6639 Part 4. Water is mixed with sodium silicate, ammonium nitrate, or glycerol to create a solution with a relative density based on the type of grain being tested. The grain sample is stirred in thoroughly and after a set time, a skimmer is used to remove floating grains. The separated grains are then examined for signs of insect infestations \cite{ISO6639Cereals1986}.

The cracking and floating method, also useful for unprocessed grains, involves crushing a sample of the stored product, immersing it in liquid with a suitable specific gravity, stirred for a set time, and allowed to settle \cite{thindFlotationTechniqueQuantitative1979,thindModifiedFlotationTechnique1984,thindDeterminationLowLevels2000}. The American Association of Cereal Chemists (AACC) method 28-22 specifies counting whole insects, cast skins, and insect fragments that float to the surface correlating it to the level of infestation \cite{braderComparisonScreeningMethods2002}.

This method was demonstrated by using durum wheat infested with \textit{Sitophilus granarius}. The material was milled to different particle sizes and submerged in different percentages of ethanol mixed with petroleum ether and mineral oil. A \SI{60}{\percent} ethanol solution enabled the easiest and fastest recovery of over \SI{90}{\percent} of the insect fragments in both bran and fine bran samples with no difference between the petroleum or mineral oil \cite{germinaraDevelopmentFlotationMethod2010}. In a different study, the cracking and floating method demonstrated a recovery rate comparable to X-ray and immunoassay methods when detecting \textit{Sitophilus granarius} in wheat \cite{braderComparisonScreeningMethods2002}.

The insect fragment test, AACC method 28-41A, is a variation of the flotation method that is used to detect insect fragments in processed products such as flour. The sample is mixed with water and mineral oil, heated, and then filtered through a sieve. The residue is then examined under a microscope for insect fragments \cite{braderComparisonScreeningMethods2002}.

While flotation methods are fast and have a wider application for detecting foreign material beyond insects, they are destructive to the sample and require specialized calibration for the specific grain type.

\subsubsection{Chemical Methods}

Chemical sensors monitor changes in the material composition or in the surrounding environment that can indicate the existence of insects, fungi, or mites.

\textit{Carbon Dioxide Analysis}

An increase in carbon dioxide ($\text{CO}_2$) levels can indicate the existence of insects respire in the stored product. The gas level can be measured through chemical absorption, gas chromatography, and electronic sensors \cite{zhangSitedirectedCO2Detection2014}.

The ISO 6639 standard describes measuring the production of $\text{CO}_2$ through the gasometric detector and the rate of $\text{CO}_2$ production through the infra-red based system. Both methods require incubation of the sample for \SI{24}{\hour} at \SI{25}{\degreeCelsius}. Results under a threshold indicate no infestation. As the $\text{CO}_2$ concentration increases, the level of infestation is categorized as low, medium, or high. At low levels, the grain is unsuitable for storage longer than two months without treatment. At medium levels, immediate fumigation is required and at high levels, the grain is considered to be heavily infested and should be treated immediately or destroyed \cite{ISO6639Cereals1986}.

$\text{CO}_2$ monitoring was implemented in Chinese grain storage facilities where aspiration pumps arranged across sampling points within the grain bulks distributed air samples to a dual-channel non-dispersive infrared (NDIR) sensor. The system was able to localize insect and mold infestations to a horizontal and vertical distance of \SI{2}{\meter} within \SI{4000}{\tonne}, \SI{30}{\meter} long and \SI{6}{\meter} high grain bulks \cite{zhangSitedirectedCO2Detection2014}. Although an increased $\text{CO}_2$ concentration above 500 parts per million (ppm) can indicate pest activity, it cannot differentiate the type of pest or level of infestation, but could instead be used as an early indicator of spoilage \cite{thiruppathiContinuousCarbonDioxide2024}.

Even though carbon dioxide analysis is non-destructive, low-cost, and can be used as an early indicator of infestation especially in large long-term storage facilities, it is not a realistic approach for inspection at ports of entry where the product is in transit and immediate results are required.

\textit{Volatile Substance Detection Method}

Insects release volatile organic compounds (VOCs) while inhabiting a product, including amino acids, pheromones, quinones, and other secretions. Amino acids are released as byproducts of digestion, excretion, and molting \cite{rajeshEfficacyTnauStackProbe2015, srivastavaFuzzyControllerBased2019, srivastavaProbabilisticArtificialNeural2019}.

Colorimetric analysis measures the presence of specific compounds through a color change reaction. The ISO 6639 standard describes a ninhydrin colorimetric method for amino acid detection. In this method, a grain sample is crushed, and amino acids from insects react with ninhydrin-coated paper to produce a purple stain, where stain intensity is correlated with infestation level \cite{ISO6639Cereals1986}. Colorimetric sensor arrays (CSA) were used with K-nearest Neighbor (KNN) machine learning to achieve a detection rate of \SI{88}{\percent} and \SI{100}{\percent} for \textit{Sitophilus zemais} and \textit{Sitophilus oryzae} in rice, respectively \cite{heSolidphasePorphyrinBorondipyrromethene2022}.

Gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) separates volatile and semi-volatile compounds based on their chemical properties and itemizes them based on their mass-to-charge ratio. GC-MS has been used to detect volatile gases from scaly grain mite, \textit{Suidasia pontifica}, in ground corn and chicken feed \cite{gongTechnologiesSystemsPest2026}.

Volatile components can be collected through headspace analysis, where the air above the sample is analyzed for VOCs. An automatic headspace sampling system was used with GC-MS to characterize the presence of \textit{Tribolium castaneum} and \textit{Cryptolestes ferrugineus} in wheat \cite{senthilkumarCharacterizationVolatileOrganic2012}. Headspace solid-phase microextraction (HS-SPME) coupled with GC-MS was able to identify dominicalure-1 and dominicalure-2 from \textit{Rhyzopertha dominica}, benzoquinone homologs from \textit{Tribolium castaneum}, and 3-hydroxy-2-butanone from \textit{Sitophilus granarius} in wheat samples \cite{caiIdentificationBiomarkerVolatile2022}. VOCs can also be found in the frass of insects, which can be collected through Solid-Phase Microextraction (SPME). This method was used to track the aggression pheromone from the frass remains of \textit{Cryptolestes ferrugineus} inhabiting a wheat sample \cite{wongIsolationIdentificationTwo1983}.

Electronic noses (E-nose) consist of an array of gas sensors with varying sensitivities to different VOCs and pattern recognition software to analyze the sensor responses. They have been used to detect insect infestations in stored products by identifying specific VOC patterns associated with different insect species and infestation levels. The E-nose was able to discriminate \textit{R. dominica} in rice using 18 metal oxide sensors at a rate of \SI{98}{\percent}  \cite{srivastavaFuzzyControllerBased2019, srivastavaProbabilisticArtificialNeural2019}.

Overall, VOC-based detection can be rapid and sensitive especially when combined with advanced machine learning techniques to find early internal and external infestations but instrumentation cost, calibration requirements, and dependence on robust reference databases remain key limitations \cite{gongTechnologiesSystemsPest2026}.

Even though it was not developed for insect detection, the electronic nose (E-nose), which consists of gas sensors with varying sensitivities and pattern recognition software, was able to detect the volatile compounds and react to the changing electrical properties to identify an infestation of the lesser grain borer, \textit{R. dominica} in wheat \cite{srivastavaFuzzyControllerBased2019,srivastavaProbabilisticArtificialNeural2019}.

\subsubsection{Optical and Electromagnetic Methods}

Different segments within the electromagnetic spectrum can be leveraged to visualize and identify visible and hidden insects within stored products. Although mostly non-destructive and fast, these methods require specialized equipment and are often limited by the size, shape, and opacity of the inspected material \cite{neethirajanDetectionTechniquesStoredProduct2007}. The early and well-developed state of artificial intelligence and machine learning for image-recognition were rapidly adopted and integrated into these imaging systems to automate processing and enhance detection and classification performance \cite{christopherridgwayImagingHighSpeedDetection2001, devipriyar.InsectPestDetection2022}.

\textit{X-ray}

X-ray operates within the \SIrange{0.01}{10}{\nano\meter} wavelength range. The varying absorption rates of X-rays by different materials create contrast in the resulting images, known as roentgenograms, allowing inspectors to identify insects concealed within grains, fruits, and vegetables, as well as find damaged products \cite{XRayInspectionSystems2023, XRayDetectionFood2023}. Grains with higher moisture content requires higher voltage levels for adequate rendering \cite{ISO6639Cereals1986}.

In this application, imagers operate in a relatively lower energy level, between \SIrange{0.12}{12}{\kilo\electronvolt}, compared to applications in medicine and tomography, to minimize damage to the inspected product while still providing sufficient penetration and contrast to achieve an image resolution to visualize hidden insects within stored products and is effective at identifying living and dead eggs, larva, pupae, and adults \cite{gongTechnologiesSystemsPest2026}. The ISO 6639 standard sets the maximum X-ray power consumption at \SI{2}{\kilo\watt} \cite{ISO6639Cereals1986}.

X-ray imaging was able to identify \textit{Sitophilus oryzae} larvae in wheat kernels with an accuracy of \SI{95}{\percent} and a detection rate of \SI{99}{\percent} for pupae and adults \cite{karunakaranSoftXrayInspection2003}. The method was able to distinguish between \textit{Rhyzopertha dominica} and \textit{Tribolium castaneum} larvae in wheat with a classification accuracy of \SI{98}{\percent} and \SI{86}{\percent}, respectively \cite{karunakaranIdentificationWheatKernels2004,karunakaranDetectionInternalWheat2004}.

The method can be automated to process large volumes of product. An automated X-ray tube based system was able to image wheat kernels with a throughput of 2500 kernels per second to detect the \textit{Sitophilus granarius} with a detection rate of \SIrange{84.4}{90.2}{\percent} \cite{haffRealtimeXrayInspection2004}. Sesotec offers a commercially available X-ray system that can process \SI{13500}{\kilo\gram\per\hour} of packaged and unpackaged food products for foreign bodies  \cite{XRayInspectionSystems2023}.

Machine learning algorithms, can further enhance the automated detection and classification performance of X-ray imaging systems. Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) was able to differentiate between \SI{86}{\percent} of uninfested and \SI{84}{\percent} of infested soybeans with all developmental stages of the cowpea beetle, \textit{Callosobruchus maculatus} \cite{chelladuraiDetectionCallosobruchusMaculatus2014}. A Support Vector Machine (SVM) classifier with a generative adversarial network (GAN) based image augmentation technique was able to detect \textit{C. maculatus} in green gram grains with an accuracy of \SI{91}{\percent} \cite{divyanthIdentificationGreenGram2022}.

While X-ray imaging is effective at detecting hidden insects, providing high certainty and reliability to provide complete visibility of the pests, their maturity and location, the structure of the grain,  and can be automated to process large volumes of product, it is expensive, requires specialized equipment and trained personnel to operate, and poses risks to human operators and the inspected product vitality from radiation exposure \cite{neethirajanDetectionTechniquesStoredProduct2007,zhuCurrentProgressInnovative2022}.

\newpage
\textit{Visible Light}

Electro-optical cameras capture images in the visible spectrum from \SIrange{400}{700}{\nano\meter} wavelength and are able to visualize the surface features, color variations, and morphological characteristics of the material and any insects present. These cameras can be color or monochrome and operate through charged coupled device (CCD) or complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) technology. Although illumination through backlighting or front lighting can enhance the contrast of the image, the opacity of the material can limit the visibility of hidden insects. Cameras can be mounted or integrated as flatbed scanners, but the scope of the image is still confined to a surface layer of the material \cite{vithuMachineVisionSystem2016}.

Machine learning algorithms have been extensively applied to visible spectrum images. The RGBInsect dataset was published featuring almost 4000 images of ten different insects species commonly found in stored products using an automated insect image acquisition device that could be fitted on insect traps as well as smartphone cameras \cite{liConstructionDatasetStoredgrain2019}. Although initially, a Faster Region-based Convolutional Neural Network (Faster R-CNN) was used to detect and classify the insect species in the dataset, the model was enhanced by using a deep convolutional neural network to augment the dataset for an accuracy of \SI{94.77}{\percent} \cite{liMultiscaleDetectionStoredgrain2020}.

Biophoton detection methods capture the bioluminescence, natural production of light, emitted by insects during metabolic activity or stress. The biophoton emission, although within the visible spectrum, is less than \SI{1e-15}{\watt\per\centi\meter\squared}. A cooled charge-coupled device (CCD) camera was used to capture the biophoton emission of the large swallowtail butterfly caterpillar, \textit{Papilio protenor}, as a two-dimensional image \cite{usuiNoninvasiveVisualizationPhysiological2019}. An ultraweak luminescence instrument, BPCL-ZL-TGC, was used with Backpropogation (Backward Propagation of Errors) neural networks to detect \textit{Sitophilus zeamais} in wheat kernels \cite{shiEfficientDetectionInternal2016}. Biological Photon Analysis Technology (BPAT) can also apply to monitor for mold growth. This method requires pretreatment of the material and is currently in the experimental phase for the limited application \cite{gongTechnologiesSystemsPest2026}.

Usage of visible spectrum imaging is relatively low-cost, non-destructive, and has a well-developed state of machine learning algorithms for image recognition and automation. However, it is limited by the opacity of the material and the visibility of the insect, which can be influenced by the insect size, life stage, and location within the material. It is also susceptible to variations in lighting conditions and repeatability.

\newpage
\textit{Near-Infared Spectroscopy and Hyperspectral Imaging}

The near-infrared (NIR) spectrum ranges from \SIrange{0.75}{2500}{\micro\meter} wavelength. NIR spectroscope leverages the unique light absorption and reflectivity properties of materials to provide information about the chemical composition and physical properties of the material, which can be affected by the presence of insects. The NIR spectrum is sensitive to changes in moisture content, protein levels, and other chemical properties that can indicate the presence of insects or their damage to the stored product \cite{neethirajanDetectionTechniquesStoredProduct2007, elizabethb.maghirangDetectingSingleWheat2002, keszthelyiNonDestructiveImagingSpectroscopic2020}.

Rice grains infested with \textit{Rhyzopertha dominica} and \textit{Sitophilus oryzae} were inspected with \SI{93.1}{\percent} accuracy using FT-NIR spectroscopy with hierarchical cluster analysis (HCA) \cite{srivastavaFuzzyControllerBased2019}. NIR analysis of rice grains using a range from \SIrange{1000}{2500}{\nano\meter} was able to distinguish healthy grain from those infested with \textit{Plodia interpunctella} with a classification rate of \SI{95.6}{\percent} and \SI{97.5}{\percent}, respectively \cite{biancolilloDeterminationInsectInfestation2019}. A shield bug, also known as the sunn pest or corn bug, \textit{Eurygaster integriceps}, infestation in wheat was found with an accuracy rate of \SI{100}{\percent} through a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and Soft Independent Modeling of Class Analogy (SIMCA) model applied to NIR spectra from \SIrange{350}{1000}{\nano\meter} \cite{basatiDetectionSunnPestdamaged2018}.

NIR spectroscopy is non-destructive and fast, but it requires constant recalibration for different grain types and kernel sizes to maintain accuracy. It is also susceptible to variations in moisture content, temperature, and other environmental factors that can affect the spectral properties of the material \cite{neethirajanDetectionTechniquesStoredProduct2007}.

Hyperspectral imaging (HSI) combines imaging and spectroscopy, specifically within the \SIrange{400}{2500}{\nano\meter} wavelength range, to capture two-dimensional spatial hypercubes where each pixel contains the chemical nature at the specific location. Using a wide range of wavelengths overwhelms the system with irrelevant information, so either bands are limited to specific ranges or feature selection methods are applied to compress the data \cite{chenHyperspectralReflectanceImaging2018}. A multi-angle NRI-HSI, within the \SIrange{866.4}{1701.0}{\nano\meter} range, was used to detect \textit{Sitophilus oryzae} in wheat kernels with an accuracy of \SI{97}{\percent} \cite{zhangIdentificationRiceweevilSitophilus2021}. An artificial neural network (ANN) was with two wavelength extraction methods, the successive projections algorithm (SPA) and the ant colony optimization (ACO), to optimize the 688 variables of a full spectral band to eight and demonstrated a \SI{98}{\percent} accuracy in detecting the mould mite, \textit{Tyrophagus putrescentiae}, and \textit{Cheyletus eruditus} in flour \cite{heDetectionMitesTyrophagus2020}.

Although HSI is rapid, non-destructive, and generates highly detailed images, it has weak penetrating ability and has not been fully developed beyond laboratory applications for insect detection in stored products.

\textit{Thermal Imaging}

Insect respiration, at all life stages, and resulting heat production would result in higher temperature levels compared to their surrounding material. While grain respires at rate of \SI{0.01}{\watt\per\tonne}, heat produced by insects can range from \SIrange{13}{81}{\micro\watt} per insect \cite{manickavasaganThermalImagingDetect2008}.

% Thermal and moisture monitoring can be conducted through sensors, such as thermocouples and thermistors, that are inserted into the material to measure the temperature at different depths and locations but they require physical contact with the material, limited to specific locations, and require a time duration to capture temperature changes \cite{a.manickavasaganThermalImagingStored2006}. 


Thermal cameras capture the infrared radiation, or heat signature, ranging from near-infrared (NIR), from \SIrange{0.75}{1.4}{\micro\meter} wavelength, to far-infrared (FIR), above \SI{15}{\micro\meter} wavelength \cite{manickavasaganThermalImagingDetect2008}. The image, known as a thermograph, is generated as the infrared radiation from the material is captured as an electrical output and processed to create a visual representation of the temperature distribution across the surface of the material. The thermal anomalies, or hot spots, can indicate the presence of insect infestations as they generate more heat compared to their surroundings \cite{bangaTechniquesInsectDetection2018}.

While thermal imaging was able to classify sound kernels from those infested by six different developmental stages of the \textit{Cryptolestes ferrugineus} within the germ of wheat kernels with a classification accuracy of \SI{77.7}{\percent} and \SI{83.5}{\percent}, respectively, it was not able to identify the specific developmental stage of the insect \cite{manickavasaganThermalImagingDetect2008}. When applied to galvanized steel silos, thermal imaging was limited to finding hot spots \SI{0.3}{\meter} from the silo wall and below the grain surface. It failed to detect hot spots with wind speeds above \SI{1}{\meter\per\second} \cite{a.manickavasaganThermalImagingStored2006}. Thermal imaging lens were used to examine the feasibility of rapid monitoring of maize, especially in large agricultural areas with UAVs, but was ineffective due to atmospheric conditions \cite{chenResearchPestImage2018}

Thermal imaging, although non-invasive and capable of providing real-time results, is limited by its inability to detect infestations below the grain surface.

\textit{Microwave}

Microwaves operate within the \SIrange{1}{1000}{\milli\meter} wavelength range. Although irradiation of stored product proves to be an effective method for controlling insect infestations, lower energy microwave systems can be used to detect the movement of insects through a material without damaging the product \cite{shayestehMortalityBehaviourTwo1996}.

A \SI{24}{\giga\hertz} microwave Doppler radar, originally used for termite detection, can also find stored product pests. The insect movement is detected by radiating a microwave signal and observing the change in the frequency of the received reflection, which increases as the insects move slightly closer or decreases as they move further away from the sensor. The system was able to detect the cigarette beetle, \textit{Lasioderma serricorne}, the black carpet beetle, \textit{Attagenus unicolor}, \textit{Tribolium castaneum}, and \textit{Oryzaephilus surinamensis}, within a corn meal and flour mixture \cite{mankinMicrowaveRadarDetection2004}. Single adult \textit{T. castaneum} and \textit{C. ferrugineus} were also detected in wheat samples up to \SI{2.1}{\centi\meter} and \SI{1.2}{\centi\meter}, respectively, from an active microwave resonator operating in \SIrange{3}{4}{\giga\hertz} range with a quality factor of 21,600 \cite{reimerCompactMicrowaveDevice2018}.

Microwave systems are potentially non-destructive but are in their infancy stage for applications towards stored product insect detection and are limited by the size of the insect, the distance between the insect and the sensor, and the material properties.

\section{Acoustic Methods for Detecting Insects in Stored Products}

Insects generate sounds within the material during locomotion through friction between the body and the material as well as through the movement of the wings, legs, and other body parts. Sounds are also generated by mastication, communication, and procreation.

The insect activity manifests in acoustic recordings as impulses, high magnitude signals across a wide frequency range and short duration which can be characterized by their intensity, duration, and spectral features. The production and magnitude of these sounds are strongly affected by the insect size and life stage, feeding habits, speed, activity, the density of the infestation, and the general behavior of the subject which can vary with temperature and external disturbance \cite{mankinPerspectivePromiseCentury2011}.

The propagation of the sound is dependent on the material properties including density, kernel size and hardness, relative humidity, and the amount of air in the material \cite{mankinPerspectivePromiseCentury2011}. The distance between the insect and the sensor as well as variation in the recording equipment, measurement methodology, and environmental conditions can also introduce differences in the results from different studies and publications.

Sensors including microphones, piezoelectric sensors and accelerometers, ultrasonic transducers, laser vibrometers, micromechanical sensors (MEMS), and seismic sensors have all been effective at capturing these bioacoustic signals (BAS) \cite{changBioacousticDetectionFour2025}. The captured signals are processed through a series of steps including noise reduction, feature extraction, and classification to used by detection algorithms to determine the presence of an insect, monitor for hidden infestations without destroying the product, classify specific species and their respective life stage, and quantify the magnitude of the infestation \cite{mankinAutomatedApplicationsAcoustics2021}.

A challenge to acoustic detection is from ambient and environmental external noise and vibrations which can be generated by machinery, human activity, and other sources. These noises can interfere with the detection of insect sounds and lead to false positives or negatives. To mitigate this issue, platforms can be designed to minimize the impact of external noise, such as by using isolation chambers, soundproofing materials, or placing sensors in locations that are less susceptible to noise. Signal processing techniques, such as filtering and noise reduction algorithms, can also be applied to enhance the signal-to-noise ratio and improve the accuracy of insect detection. Additional sensors have also been implemented to monitor for environmental noise and remove its influence on the results.

Acoustic inspection systems have been developed for different applications, such as in-field monitoring, port of entry inspection, and long-term storage facilities. These platforms are designed as standalone receptacles, probes, and mounted sensors for momentary examination or continuous monitoring. These systems have been automated to alleviate the need for trained personnel to operate and interpret the results, and to provide real-time results for rapid inspection.

Often the sensors, signal features, processing, and algorithms are common with the platforms, analysis, and detection methods for insect infestations in other materials such as plants, soil, and wood \cite{sutinAutomatedAcousticDetection2019}.

\subsection{Acoustic Signatures of Insects in Stored Products}

\subsubsection{Spectral Features}

The spectral domain refers to the frequency content of a sound. Generally, insect noise occurs in the frequency band between \SIrange{80}{8000}{\hertz} \cite{mankinPerspectivePromiseCentury2011} but can reach into the ultrasonic range up to \SI{75}{\kilo\hertz} \cite{klaassenCharacterizationUltrasonicSignals1988} and can vary between the species and life stages \cite{azizifarsaniBioacousticsTrogodermaGranarium2023,fleurat-lessardAcousticDetectionAutomatic2006}. The khapra beetle, \textit{T. granarium}, in wheat grains, was most dominant in frequencies \SIrange{1000}{8000}{\kilo\hertz}. The adult and larva occupied different bands, \SIrange{3500}{4500}{\hertz} and \SIrange{1000}{1700}{\hertz}, respectively. Female adults dominated mainly in the \SIrange{1000}{3000}{\hertz} and \SIrange{4000}{5000}{\hertz} frequency bands \cite{azizifarsaniBioacousticsTrogodermaGranarium2023}. The peak energies for an adult and larva of the rice weevil, \textit{Sitophilus oryzae}, in wheat grains were within \SIrange{1800}{3000}{\hertz} and \SIrange{1300}{2000}{\hertz}, respectively \cite{fleurat-lessardAcousticDetectionAutomatic2006}. Variety in frequency content was used to distinguish between the larval and adult stages of the confused flour beetle, \textit{Tribolium confusum}, \textit{S. granarius}, and the \textit{R. dominica} through spectral features alone \cite{schwabAutomaticAcousticalSurveillance2005}.

\subsubsection{Temporal Characteristics}

The temporal domain refers to the time-varying nature of the signal including amplitude and duration. A single impulse, often termed as event, might be \SIrange{3}{30}{\milli\second}. Bursts, which are a series of pulses that can be enumerated by their number and rate within a time period, can range from 50 to 2000 impulses for a duration from \SIrange{3}{30}{\milli\second} with up to \SI{250}{\milli\second} intervals of separation \cite{klaassenCharacterizationUltrasonicSignals1988}. Adult \textit{R. dominica} and \textit{T. castaneum} produced 37 and 80 times more sound than their larvae \cite{mankinPerspectivePromiseCentury2011}. The pulse size and duration can also be used to differentiate between species and sizes, where larger insects produce impulses with higher amplitude and longer duration \cite{changBioacousticDetectionFour2025}. An increase in impulses could be indicative of a higher population infesting the material \cite{klaassenCharacterizationUltrasonicSignals1988,hagstrumAutomatedAcousticalMonitoring1991}. The number of events also increase with the lifespan of the insects \cite{klaassenCharacterizationUltrasonicSignals1988}. Some algorithms count the number of bursts and impulses to determine an infestation \cite{mankinAcousticIndicatorsTargeted2010,mankinPerspectivePromiseCentury2011, njorogeAcousticPitfallTrap2019, mankinAutomatedApplicationsAcoustics2021}.

\subsubsection{Behavioral Influences}

The different behaviors of the insects, such as feeding, mating, and communication, can generate different acoustic signatures. During feeding, the sound is characterized by clicks and pulses of the striking mandibles. The sounds generated by insects in locomotion through the material are more broadband, perceived as rustling, wriggling, scratching, and scraping \cite{mankinAcousticIndicatorsTargeted2010}. The different temporal and spectral characteristics of these behaviors have been used to differentiate the two \cite{mousaviAcousticDetectionPossibility2017}. Some insects also produce sounds for communication, such as stridulation made by the \textit{Sitophilus oryzae} in the \SIrange{1800}{3000}{\hertz} range as a defense and territoriality mechanism \cite{gibsonStridulatoryMechanismsSound1967}, or through calls and vibrations such as those made by the Asian corn borer moth, \textit{Ostrinia furnacalis}, which can reach \SI{38}{\deci\bel} SPL above \SI{100000}{\hertz} \cite{nakanoMothsAreNot2009}. The quality of the acoustic signal can also be an indication of the life stage of the insect and level of infestation. Larvae often produce lower amplitude pulses at smaller bursts compared to adults \cite{njorogeFrequencyTimePattern2016, njorogeBioacousticsAcanthoscelidesObtectus2017}. During molting or diapause, insects enter stages of quiescence, where they are less active and produce fewer sounds \cite{shadeDetectionHiddenInsect1990,mankinThermalTreatmentsIncrease1999,njorogeOxygenConsumptionAcoustic2018}.

\newpage
\subsubsection{Environmental Considerations}

Insect activity and the resulting acoustic signals can be affected by the surrounding environment. Larval movement and feeding decreases at lower temperatures. Heat treatment of the material would increase the resulting acoustic signals, improving speed and reliability of detection. Grain infested with \textit{Sitophilus oryzae} initially kept at standard \SIrange{11}{17}{\degreeCelsius} storage temperature had 2-30 times higher insect activity when exposed to radiant and convective heat treatments, than the controlled untreated samples \cite{mankinThermalTreatmentsIncrease1999}.

Disturbances in the insect environment can also affect the timing and magnitude of the generated acoustic signals. Moving infested materials, such as during relocation into a sensor or from severe vibration, resulted in no or decreased insect generated noise \cite{klaassenCharacterizationUltrasonicSignals1988}. The ISO standard guides the operator to wait for at least \SI{5}{\minute} for grains to stabilize before monitoring for insect sounds \cite{ISO6639Cereals1986}.

\subsubsection{Material Effects}

The propagation of the sound through the granular material is dependent on the substrate properties including density, kernel size and hardness, relative humidity and moisture content, and the amount of air gaps \cite{mankinPerspectivePromiseCentury2011}.

Beans typically consists of two curved halves, mostly a homogeneous cotyledon in physical contact with an outer edge and a large air cavity in the center. Sound generated on one side of the bean must travel around the air cavity, resulting in a delay and attenuation of the signal between the opposite sides. The water content of the bean, influenced by the surrounding environment and weather, can also vary the material properties such as the density and modulus of elasticity, resulting in frequency and phase shifts \cite{klaassenCharacterizationUltrasonicSignals1988}.

Two Br\"{u}el \& Kj\ae r microphones with a \SI{13}{\milli\meter} diameter measured the sound transmission of a speaker through \SI{5}{\meter\cubed} of soft and hard wheat, brown rice, soybeans, corn, and sorghum. The sound transmission was highly dependent on the average spacing between the kernels. Tortuosity describes the porosity of the material and the complexity of the path the sound must travel through. Larger grains, such as corn and soybeans, have significantly wider passageways between kernels which result in smaller acoustic absorption coefficients, enabling the sound to travel further. Smaller grains, such as wheat, sorghum, and brown rice, have relatively smaller kernel sizes and as a result are more tightly packed with narrower passageways. The tighter spacing leads to higher acoustic attenuation. The wheat acted as a low-pass filter, highly absorbing frequencies above \SI{1000}{\hertz} over a distance of \SI{1}{\meter}. Average spacing between kernels decreased with depth in a bulk grain, where the change in porosity and tortuosity would result in different distances for the sound waves to travel, altering scattering rates at grain interfaces, and energy dissipation as a result of the multiple reflections and refraction \cite{hicklingSoundTransmissionStored1995,hicklingStudiesSoundTransmission1997}.

\subsection{Design of Acoustic Detection Systems}

The design of the acoustic detection system encompasses the choice of hardware, software, and their integration into a platform for insect detection. The system architecture is guided by the specific deployment and application with consideration for environmental conditions.

\subsubsection[Sensors for Detecting Acoustic Signals Generated by Stored Product Pests]{Sensors for Detecting Acoustic Signals Generated by SPPs}

\textit{Traditional Microphones}

Traditional microphones convert sound waves in the air into electrical signals. In stored product insect detection, these microphones are often used to capture the sounds emitted by the insects as they displace material, feed, or vocalize.

Microphones can monitor the sounds within the room in which the target is located \cite{brandingInsectSound1000InsectSound2024} or be used in a contact manner, such as in the Portable Postharvest Detection System (PDS), which integrated an electret condenser microphone into a plexiglass base on which the grain sample is placed \cite{mankinPerformanceLowCostAcoustic2020}. Microphones can also be inserted directly into the substrate \cite{njorogeFrequencyTimePattern2016}. Alternatively, microphones can be separated from the testing material through an air gap, such as in a system design that features a microphone mounted in a system body which can interface through a decoupling tube to bespoke containers for stored product as well as fruit and vegetable samples \cite{vickSoundDetectionStoredProduct1988,webbAcousticalSystemDetect1988}.

Another application is through impact acoustics, which instead of measuring insect sounds, detects the differences in sounds generated by whole and damaged kernels. Microphones are placed near a metallic plate and differentiate the sounds radiated by the strikes of whole and damaged kernels as they are individually dropped through a single-file feeder \cite{bueranoMicrophoneSystemOptimization2012,pearsonFeasibilityImpactacousticEmissions2007}.

\textit{Piezoelectric Sensors}

Piezoelectric materials convert mechanical stress produced by the elastic waves, generated by insects and propagated through granular materials, into electrical signals.

Thickness-Shear Mode (TSM) resonators consist of a piezoelectric disc positioned between two electrodes. Shear waves, generated by the mass-loading effect of the insect movement in the material, induce deformations in the thickness direction of the disc \cite{frenchLateralFieldExcited2007}. Surface Acoustic Wave (SAW) sensors meanwhile translate the sound waves that propagate longitudinally along the surface of the disc. Shear-horizontal Acoustic Plate Mode (SHAPM) measure the waves that travel through the thickness of the disc and are more sensitive to the velocity, phase, and attenuation even on the outermost layer of the substrate in which they are embedded \cite{loganathanNonchemicalMethodsDisinfestation2025}.

The piezoelectric sensors can be integrated into acoustic probes, such as the Early Warning Detector (EWD) \cite{fleurat-lessardAcousticDetectionAutomatic2006}, which can be inserted into the material \cite{eliopoulosDetectionAdultBeetles2015,changBioacousticDetectionFour2025}. Systems such as Acoustic Location Fingerprinting Insect Detector (ALFID) \cite{shumanQuantitativeAcousticalDetection1993} are designed as standalone receptacles with embedded piezoelectric sensors where the product is placed momentarily during examination. Polyvinylidene Fluoride (PVDF) use piezoelectric polymers which can be fabricated into thin films and are more flexible and adaptable for mounting \cite{mankinBugBytesSound2019}.

\textit{Accelerometers}

Accelerometers sense the vibrations in up to three orthogonal axes. The TreeVibes system was developed as a probe with a uniaxial accelerometer to detect wood-boring insects in trees but was explored to detect \textit{Sitophilus oryzae} eggs, larvae, and adults in \SI{300}{\milli\liter} wheat grains over a weeklong period \cite{potamitisInVivoVibroacousticSurveillance2019, rigakisTreeVibesModernTools2021}. Another study considered an accelerometer attached to wheat stems in order to detect the wheat stem sawfly, \textit{Cephus cinctus}, and the Mexican bromeliad weevil, \textit{Metamasius callizona}, demonstrating its potential usage in farming stages \cite{mankinAcousticSystemInsect2004, mankinAutomatedApplicationsAcoustics2021}.


\textit{Ultrasonic Sensors}

Traditional microphones, piezoelectric sensors, and accelerometers can be extended to support ultrasonic frequencies above \SI{20000}{\hertz}. A condenser microphone was used to capture the stridulation of the merchant grain beetle, \textit{Oryzaephilus mercator}, the flat grain beetle, \textit{Cryptolestes pusillus}, the furniture carpet beetle, \textit{Anthrenus flavipes}, the glabrous cabinet beetle, \textit{Trogoderma glabrum}, the cacao moth, \textit{Ephestia elutella}, the almond moth, \textit{Cadra cautella}, and \textit{Sitophilus granarius} in the \SIrange{15000}{60000}{\hertz} range \cite{wojcikTestsAudibleUltrasonic1968}.

The Ultrasonic Insect Detector, patented in 1989, is an active insect detection system that radiates and receives ultrasonic pulses. It uses a half-inch diameter piezoelectric transducer with a center frequency of \SI{40000}{\hertz}. The system was used to detect \textit{C. maculatus} larvae internally feeding inside cowpeas \cite{shadeUltrasonicInsectDetector1989,shadeDetectionHiddenInsect1990}. Although not specifically for stored product insects, ultrasonic emitters and receivers were used to detect insects and damage in wood by measuring changes in the time of flight (TOF) and ultrasonic spectrum attenuation \cite{liuReviewRecentSensing2017}.

\textit{Micromechanical Sensors (MEMS)}

Advances and increased accessibility to micro-electromechanical systems (MEMS), which miniaturize traditional, piezoelectric, and accelerometer sensors, have enabled the development of compact, low-power, and cost-effective acoustic detection systems \cite{balingbingDeterminingSoundSignatures2024, keerthanaAcousticDetectionCallosobruchus2025}.

\textit{Laser Doppler Vibrometers (LDV)}

Laser Doppler Vibrometers (LDV) are non-contact sensors that use laser beams to measure the velocity and displacement of vibrating surfaces. They can capture the vibrations caused by insect activity on the surface of the material without physical contact, making them suitable for delicate or hard-to-reach areas where a traditional sensor cannot be placed. They are also less affected by the influence of external noise and vibrations compared to contact sensors \cite{liuReviewRecentSensing2017}.

Sensors such as the PDV-100 vibrometer can measure distances up to \SI{30}{\meter} and a frequency range from \SIrange{0}{22000}{\hertz} \cite{liuReviewRecentSensing2017}. The vibrometer was used to detect the larger cabinet beetle, \textit{Trogoderma inclusum}, and darkling beetle, \textit{Tenebrio molitor}, in wheat samples but was limited to a distance of \SI{50}{\milli\meter} \cite{flynnAcousticMethodsInvasive2016}. LDVs have been explored for detecting insects in trees and wood-products, plants, fruits and vegetables, and soil \cite{mankinPerspectivePromiseCentury2011, sutinVibroacousticMethodsInsect2017, liuReviewRecentSensing2017, bhairaviRecentAdvancesExploration2020, mankinAutomatedApplicationsAcoustics2021, loganathanNonchemicalMethodsDisinfestation2025}.

\subsubsection{Signal Processing}

After the acoustic signals are captured by the sensors, they are processed through hardware and software to enhance the signal quality and extract relevant features for analysis and detection. While hardware processing can be done through analog filters and amplifiers enabling low-latency and real-time processing, software allows for more complex and customizable processing techniques to the detriment of introducing latency, digital artifacts, and requiring more computational resources \cite{mankinAutomatedApplicationsAcoustics2021}. Digital signal processing (DSP) is highly dependent on sampling rate which, based on the Nyquist theorem, must be at least twice the highest frequency of interest to avoid aliasing and loss of information. Higher sampling rates, such as those required for ultrasonic frequencies, require more processing power and storage capacity \cite{mankinPerspectivePromiseCentury2011}.

Preliminary signal processing steps include amplification, such as through the Acoustic Emission Amplifier (AED-2010L), which increases the strength of the relatively weak insect-generated signals \cite{eliopoulosEstimationPopulationDensity2016}. Filters and transformations can be used to further isolate the frequency range of interest and expose relevant signals while removing superfluous noise that can vary between sensors and environments. The output from the processing presents data sufficient for detection algorithms to determine presence of an insect.

The waveform envelope allows for threshold crossing algorithms to determine an insect presence if the amplitude exceeds a statistically or empirically determined cutoff \cite{mankinAutomatedApplicationsAcoustics2021}. The sounds of \textit{Sitophilus oryzae} movement and feeding were observed to be at an amplitude of \SI{23}{\deci\bel} \cite{mankinNoiseShieldingAcoustic1996}. Another threshold algorithm included continuous recording of the \textit{R. dominica}, the \textit{S. oryzae}, and the \textit{T. castaneum} in rice over a \SI{24}{\hour} time frame using an Adafruit 12S MEMS SPH0645 microphone and calculated the root-mean-square (RMS) of \SI{3}{\minute} windows at each hour of a signal filtered between \SIrange{93.75}{2500}{\hertz} \cite{balingbingDeterminingSoundSignatures2024}. A \SI{14}{\centi\meter} long piezoelectric sensor was inserted with a probe into hard wheat grain. The system was able to accurately detect the presence of an insect with a probability range from \SIrange{72}{100}{\percent}. The algorithm was based on extracting the Hilbert envelope and indicating a detection when it meant a certain threshold \cite{eliopoulosDetectionAdultBeetles2015}.

\subsubsection{Machine Learning Applications}

Advances in machine learning (ML) algorithms in recent years demonstrated new opportunities for insect detection, quantification, and classification. ML algorithms can analyze acoustic signals and identify patterns associated with insect activity as well as distinguish external noise, but they depend on large amounts of training data. The ML models can be trained on the raw audio signals or on extracted features, such as spectrograms, and the data can be provided in labeled or unlabeled forms \cite{faissAdaptiveRepresentationsSound2023, kohlbergBuzzesBytesSystematic2024}. These algorithms include various neural network classifiers such as convolution neural networks (CNN), probabilistic neural networks (PNN), perceptual learning prediction (PLP), decision trees and forests, hidden Markov models (HMM), support vector machines (SVM), and Bayesian classifiers \cite{mainettiAcousticIdentificationWoodBoring,potamitisAutomaticBioacousticDetection2009,mankinAutomatedApplicationsAcoustics2021, balingbingDeterminingSoundSignatures2024}. Deep learning combines multiple layers of neural networks to learn the features and patterns of the data. Since machine learning algorithms are data-driven, the performance of the model might not generalize to new species or materials, if the training data is not diverse enough.

\subsubsection{Platform Design and Deployment}

The platform of an acoustic system refers to the physical design and deployment of the sensor. There are a variety of platform styles for acoustic detection of stored product insects. Some systems are designed as probes that can be inserted into the material, which enables extended deployment in storage facilities for continuous monitoring and early detection of infestations \cite{hagstrumAcousticalMonitoringRhyzopertha1990, fleurat-lessardAcousticDetectionAutomatic2006,muller-blenkleMethodAcousticStorage2023}. A more common design features a standalone receptacle with embedded sensors where the product is placed momentarily during examination \cite{vickSoundDetectionStoredProduct1988,webbAcousticalSystemDetect1988, shumanQuantitativeAcousticalDetection1993, mankinAutomatedApplicationsAcoustics2021}. Sensors can also be mounted on the storage containers \cite{mankinPerspectivePromiseCentury2011}.

Integration of multiple acoustic sensors simultaneously in a platform could increase detection performance, determine locations of insects, level of infestation, and to 
offset the rate and duration of monitoring \cite{mankinPerspectivePromiseCentury2011}. Deploying a sensor array achieved comparable performance as a single sensor monitored more frequently and for longer recording durations \cite{hagstrumStoredProductProtection2012}.

\textit{Mitigation of External Noise and Vibration}

One significant drawback of using acoustic sensing is its vulnerability to external noise, prevalent in expected operation environments such as storage and inspection facilities, which can mask the insect sounds and lead to false positives and negatives \cite{mankinPerspectivePromiseCentury2011}.

An initial step in limiting the effects of external noise is through the platform design by incorporating shielding, sound-proofing, and vibration isolation. Mankin and colleagues developed two large and heavyweight acoustic anechoic chambers that were applied for insect signature measurement. The first system reduced background noise by \SIrange{50}{70}{\deci\bel} from \SIrange{1000}{10000}{\hertz} by shielding a sensor with six layers of alternating plywood, loaded vinyl, and foam wedges totaling \SI{26}{\centi\meter} and weighing \SI{81}{\kilo\gram} \cite{mankinNoiseShieldingAcoustic1996}. The subsequent system incorporated a cylindrical vessel weighing \SI{70}{\kilo\gram} and suppressed \SIrange{60}{90}{\deci\bel} between \SIrange{1000}{10000}{\hertz} using a \SI{1}{\milli\meter} steel wall and \SI{50.8}{\milli\meter} of foam \cite{mankinShieldingNoiseInterfering1997}.

Double chamber designs further isolate the sensor from external noise by enclosing the sensor in an inner chamber that is separated from an outer chamber by an air gap \cite{keerthanaAcousticDetectionCallosobruchus2025}. One such system described used a double wall chamber where a \SI{50}{\milli\meter} air gap separated an inner container lined with three different compressed density foams between two \SI{18}{\milli\meter} plywood walls vertically spring mounted from the outer container featuring a wall \SI{50}{\milli\meter} of acoustic foam and \SI{18}{\milli\meter} of plywood \cite{njorogeFrequencyTimePattern2016}. Another system design used a spring suspended double wall chamber to investigate low frequency band and external vibration isolation. It featured wood paneling and acoustic foam layers that weighed \SI{620}{\kilo\gram} and was capable of sound attenuation of \SI{50}{\deci\bel} above \SI{100}{\hertz}. The microphone array inside this chamber was utilized to develop the InsectSound 100 dataset \cite{brandingNoiseRobustAcoustic2023, brandingInsectSound1000InsectSound2024}.

Additional microphones are also leveraged to capture ambient noise occurring outside the sensor. A Br\"{u}el \& Kj\ae r 4145 microphone was situated above the floor and outside the ALFID system during high noise levels from \SIrange{65}{85}{\deci\bel} to validate the sensor's noise masking triggers \cite{mankinShieldingNoiseInterfering1997}. In another study, a microphone was placed underneath a detection platform, but its usage in the algorithm was not published \cite{brandingNoiseRobustAcoustic2023}.

\subsection{Automated Acoustic Systems for Insect Detection in Stored Products}

The ISO 6639 standard describes the use of acoustic methods for detecting insects in stored products. The standard outlines using a box lined with high-performance sound insulated material, such as rock wool or high-density polyester foam with lead, that is placed on an elastic suspension system. The sample container is a thick plastic cylinder with a hermetic closure and a vibration sensor placed in the center. After applying an amplification of \SIrange{50}{100}{\deci\bel} and a band-pass filter from \SIrange{600}{4000}{\hertz}, the standard guides the operator to use headphones, loudspeaker, voltage or current threshold detections, or magnetic tape recordings to actively monitor for insect sounds. If the operator observes less than one insect sound per \SI{1}{\minute}, the sample is considered uninfested. If there is one noise per \SI{1}{\minute}, the sample is considered probably infested. Two noises determines that the sample is lightly infested and needs to be further examined. If there are more than five noises in the \SI{1}{\minute} interval, the sample is considered highly infested \cite{ISO6639Cereals1986}.

Automated acoustic insect detection systems would ideally be capable of performing real-time detection of insects with minimal human oversight and interaction, a capability gap in current methods at Ports of Entry and within the ISO 6639 acoustic detection standard. The performance of the sensors and associated algorithm are usually evaluated through their detection accuracy of correctly identifying infested and uninfested samples, the false positive rate of incorrectly identifying uninfested samples as infested, and the false negative rate of incorrectly identifying infested samples as clean. Incorrectly identifying infested samples as clean would lead to the risk of pest spread and damage, while incorrectly identifying clean samples as infested would lead to unnecessary treatment and loss of the product.

One platform featuring an acoustic microphone attached, but decoupled, to a grain container through a diaphragm and sealed air column achieved a \SIrange{71}{90}{\percent} detection rate for \textit{S. oryzae}, \textit{R. dominica}, and \textit{S. cerelella} in wheat. The signals were amplified by \SI{60}{\deci\bel}, filtered between \SIrange{200}{8000}{\hertz}, enveloped, and an amplitude threshold crossing algorithm determined the detection. The system was able to estimate the infestation rates in the range of \numrange{1}{20} kernels per \SI{100}{\milli\liter} of sample \cite{vickSoundDetectionStoredProduct1988, webbAcousticalSystemDetect1988}.

\newpage
Another system consisted of 16 piezoelectric sensors installed across four cables in a \SI{208}{\liter} steel drum to monitor two to 20 adult \textit{T. castaneum} and \textit{R. dominica} in \SI{1.5}{\kilo\gram} of wheat. Although the preliminary signal processing is not explicitly described, the authors observed a high correlation between the number of insects within \SI{100}{\milli\liter} of wheat and the number of insect sounds detected over \SI{60}{\second} intervals. Using this correlation, the authors were able to fit a linear regression model and achieve a \SI{90}{\percent} detection rate for densities from 0.04 to 0.32 insects per \SI{1}{\kilo\gram} \cite{hagstrumAcousticalMonitoringRhyzopertha1990}.

The Acoustic Location Fixing Insect Detector (ALFID) used an array of 16 piezoelectric sensors distributed vertically in a gravity loaded, \SI{76}{\centi\meter} by \SI{5}{\centi\meter} by \SI{4}{\centi\meter}, container capable of testing \SI{1}{\kilo\gram} of sample. A band-pass filter from \SIrange{1000}{10000}{\hertz} was applied to the signals and an amplitude threshold detection was used for detection. The system allows estimation of insect location based on the sensor position with maximal signal amplitude. Larva of the \textit{S. oryzae} in \SI{1}{\kilo\gram} of wheat were detected with a \SI{64}{\percent} accuracy and a false positive rate of \SI{8}{\percent}. Clean wheat samples were correctly identified as uninfested with a \SI{92}{\percent} accuracy \cite{shumanQuantitativeAcousticalDetection1993,mankinAcousticCountingAdult1997}. The system was patented in 1996 \cite{vickAcousticLocationFixing1996}.

The Early Warning Detector incorporated three levels of piezoelectric sensors into a \SI{1.4}{\meter} probe that can be inserted grain bin units. The algorithm compared the insect signal spectra to a previously collected reference database of 990 samples. The system was able to detect adults and larvae of \textit{S. oryzae} and \textit{R. dominica} at infestation levels ranging from one to ten insect larvae per \SI{10}{\kilo\gram} of grain with a confidence factor of \SI{95}{\percent} \cite{fleurat-lessardAcousticDetectionAutomatic2006}.

A \SI{14}{\centi\meter} long piezoelectric sensor mounted on a probe was used to collect signals of wheat and corn inhabited by \textit{Tribolium confusum}, \textit{Oryzaephilus surinamensis}, \textit{Cryptolestes ferrugineus}, \textit{Lasioderma serricorne}, \textit{S. oryzae}, and \textit{R. dominica} at different infestation levels. After amplifying the signals through an Acoustic Emission Amplifier (AED-2010L), the impulses were extracted using a Hilbert transformation and a threshold crossing algorithm determined a positive detection when the envelope exceeded three times the mean of \SI{80}{\percent} of the lowest amplitudes. The system achieved a \SI{100}{\percent} negative detection rate and a \SIrange{72}{100}{\percent} positive detection rate for one to two insects per kilogram depending on the species \cite{eliopoulosDetectionAdultBeetles2015}. The same system was used with different machine learning algorithms to determine infestation levels with a success rate above \SI{70}{\percent} \cite{eliopoulosEstimationPopulationDensity2016}.

The Portable Postharvest Detection System (PDS) utilized electret microphones embedded in a plexiglass base. After a band-pass filter was applied from \SIrange{20}{5000}{\hertz}, spectral matching software was used, specifically in the frequencies between \SIrange{500}{2500}{\hertz}, to compare matching sound spectra, sound rate, and other likelihood indicators to a database of known insect sounds and discriminate against background noise. The system detected \textit{S. oryzae} adults in \SI{2.6}{\kilo\gram} of grain at a rate of 1.9 insects per \si{\kilo\gram} and \textit{T. castaneum} adults at a rate of 3.8 insects per \si{\kilo\gram} of flour \cite{mankinPerformanceLowCostAcoustic2020}.

\newpage
The Beetle Sound Tube system consisted of perforated tubes placed vertically into grain bins with an insect trap at the bottom. A precision piezoelectric microphone was used for acoustic signal recording. The system was scalable and was demonstrated in ranges from a single tube in a \SI{1}{\liter} container, three tubes in a \SI{300}{\tonne} silo, to nine tubes in a flat storage container. The system's performance was detailed for \textit{Oryzaephilus surinamensis} in barley. The underlying algorithm of the system collected \SI{5}{\minute} of acoustic data every hour, divided it into blocks of 128 samples with a duration of \SI{2.7}{\milli\second}, and calculated the energy level of each block. After setting the minimum energy in a \SI{5}{\minute} recording as the background energy level, any energy blocks that exceeded this level by \SI{5}{\deci\bel} were used to determine the beginning and end of a pulse, resulting in a standardized pulse length of 1280 samples. A detection algorithm using $\frac{1}{3}$-octave band analysis enabled instantaneous detection by calculating the energy across 16 different bands from \SIrange{250}{6000}{\hertz} and comparing it to the background noise level which was determined as \SI{5}{\percent} quantile of the energy level for each band. A signal was counted as an insect event if its energy exceeded frequency-specific thresholds, ranging from \SIrange{15}{25}{\deci\bel} above background noise within bands at \SI{800}{\hertz} or higher, while signals exceeding the threshold below \SI{800}{\hertz} were discarded as noise \cite{muller-blenkleMethodAcousticStorage2023}.

Another system used a piezoelectric sensor inserted through a \SI{5}{\centi\meter} hole into a \SI{60}{\centi\meter} by \SI{30}{\centi\meter} by \SI{37}{\centi\meter} bin capable of holding \SI{10}{\kilo\gram} of material and insulated with \SI{20}{\milli\meter} aluminum alloy and \SI{40}{\milli\meter} polyurethane foam with a noise reduction coefficient (NRC) of 0.80. The sounds of ten individual Adzuki bean weevil, \textit{Callosobruchus chinensis}, seed weevil, \textit{Callosobruchus analis}, \textit{Zabrotes subfasiatus}, and \textit{C. maculatus} feeding as larva and moving as adults in red and green beans were recorded in varying temperatures from \SIrange{10}{38}{\celsius}. A Finite Impulse Response (FIR) high-pass filter was applied with a cutoff frequency of \SI{2000}{\hertz}. The number of pulses, their sizes, and durations were measured to determine that the optimal temperature was \SI{31}{\celsius} and that pulse duration was the best characteristic to distinguish between species regardless of temperature and bean type \cite{changBioacousticDetectionFour2025}.

A \SI{100}{\percent} detection rate was achieved for \textit{Sitophilus oryzae} in rice using a system with a piezoelectric sensor described in \cite{potamitisAutomaticBioacousticDetection2009}. After applying a high-pass filter from \SI{2000}{\hertz}, the linear frequency cepstral coefficients (LFCC) and discrete wavelet packet features (DWPF) were extracted from the signals. A Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) classifier was trained on the features to determine the presence of insects.

Micro-electromechanical system (MEMS) microphones, the Adafruit I2S SPH0645, were used to measure the sounds of \textit{R. dominica}, \textit{S. oryzae}, and \textit{T. castaneum} in a \SI{16}{\centi\meter} by \SI{16}{\centi\meter} by \SI{8}{\centi\meter} container filled with \SI{500}{\gram} sample of rice. After applying a band-pass filter from \SIrange{93.75}{3000}{\hertz}, the authors found identifiable differences in peak frequencies between the species as well as in the root-mean-square (RMS) energy. A convolutional neural network (CNN) model was trained on generated spectrogram images of the signal and achieved an \SI{84.51}{\percent} classification accuracy  \cite{balingbingDeterminingSoundSignatures2024, balingbingApplicationMultilayerConvolutional2024}.

\subsection{Acoustic Insect Datasets}

Several audio databases featuring insects have been published and are publicly available. The Animal Sound Archive \cite{registry-migration.gbif.orgAnimalSoundArchive2016}, InsectSet32 \cite{faissInsectSet32DatasetAutomatic2022}, InsectSet47 and InsectSet66 \cite{faissInsectSet47InsectSet66Expanded2023, faissAdaptiveRepresentationsSound2023}, InsectSound1000 \cite{brandingInsectSound1000InsectSound2024}, and Singing Insects of North America (SINA) \cite{SingingInsectsNorth2024} all include insect recordings; however, these datasets feature neither species commonly found infesting stored products nor audio recordings of insects within materials or stored product. BugBytes has 95 recordings of insects in different environments but only presents approximately 23 minutes of recordings with less than 15\% of that pertaining to stored product insects; only two of which recorded with piezoelectric disks \cite{mankinBugBytesSound2019}.

\subsection{Previous Work by the Stevens Institute of Technology}

Stevens Institute of Technology developed several iterations of acoustic sensors and algorithms to detect insects while inspecting stored products during transportation and import. They tested the sensors on live insects including the larger cabinet beetle, \textit{Trogoderma inclusum}, mealworm and darkling beetle, \textit{Tenebrio molitor}, the corn earworm, \textit{Helicoverpa zea}, \textit{Copitarsia} larvae, and the khapra beetle, \textit{Trogoderma granarium}, within a variety of materials including grains, vegetables, and herbs.

The first acoustic system comprised a test container outfitted with ceramic piezoelectric sensors that output four separate audio channels into an external preamplifier and an analog-to-digital converter (ADC) before reaching a computer for recording and post-processing. The test container is located within a large box with two plastic containers separated by sound-absorbing foam in between. The design provided from \SIrange{50}{80}{\deci\bel} of sound attenuation for frequencies above \SI{500}{\hertz}.

After determining that the acoustic signals recorded from insect presence were concentrated in the frequency band from \SIrange{400}{1000}{\hertz}, the group developed a processing chain algorithm to detect the presence of insects in the recorded signals. The signal is split into two bands, \SIrange{200}{600}{\hertz} and \SIrange{600}{1500}{\hertz}, for the noise and insect signal, respectively. The envelopes of the signals are calculated using a Hilbert transform. The difference between the two envelopes is used as a detection metric. A detection is determined when the metric exceeds a threshold value. This system was able to detect a \textit{T. inclusum} larva and adult with 100\% and 70\% accuracy, respectively, and reported a 0\% false positive rate when testing without live insects within \SI{1}{\kilo\gram} of rice \cite{flynnAcousticMethodsInvasive2016}.

Several years later, the group redesigned the apparatus to be more portable and integrated the preamplifier and data acquisition electronics into the container. A large Pelican case was modified with acoustic dampening material to provide \SI{60}{\deci\bel} of external noise dampening for frequencies above \SI{500}{\hertz}. The \SI{40}{\centi\meter} by \SI{60}{\centi\meter} testing enclosure within featured three measurement channels, each utilizing two piezoelectric modules. The updated detection algorithm involves applying a band-pass filter from \SIrange{500}{5000}{\hertz}, smoothing the squared signals through a moving time average filter with a time window of \SI{50}{\milli\second}, and normalizing to the average intensity. A detection was determined when the signal exceeded the background noise level with a \SI{5}{\deci\bel} threshold \cite{sutinVibroacousticMethodsInsect2017}.
