\chapter{Reducing Influence of External Noise}

\section{Efficacy of Physical Noise Reduction}

One of the challenges of acoustic sensing is the interference of external noise obscuring the signals of interest. In addition to physical insulation to suppress external noise and vibration, acoustic microphones were included in the design of the A-SPIDS platform to assist the detection algorithm in identifying noise as anomalies. The microphones recording over channel four and five were mounted coaxially with the piezoelectric channels zero and one, respectively, while microphones labeled as channel six and seven were placed within the insulation and externally on the system, respectively. Figure \ref{fig:external_microphones_noise} compares the spectra and signal-noise ratio of the acoustic microphones during presence of external white noise at \SI{90}{\deci\bel A} without insects present measured against a reference signal when no insect or noise were present:

\begin{figure}[!htbp]

	\caption{SNR of microphones during exposure to \SI{90}{\deci\bel A} external white noise}
	\label{fig:external_microphones_noise}
\end{figure}

The difference in strength between the microphones demonstrate the effectiveness of the insulation in muffling external noise especially at frequencies above \SI{2000}{\hertz}. The insulation alone is shown to provide an average of \SI{35}{\deci\bel} and a maximum of \SI{43}{\deci\bel} noise reduction. Although \citeauthor{mankinNoiseShieldingAcoustic1996} was able to achieve a higher level of attenuation, their solution was significantly larger and heavier compared to the compact and portable A-SPIDS \cite{mankinNoiseShieldingAcoustic1996}. The channel seven microphone was chosen as the external noise reference for the detection algorithm.

\subsection{Influence of Ambient Noise}

The high sensitivity of the piezoelectric discs make them vulnerable to external noise even through the physical insulation. Figure \ref{fig:90_spectrogram} shows the spectrograms from the four internal piezoelectric sensors and the external microphone, channel seven, during a recording with no insect and \SI{90}{\deci\bel A} of external noise: 

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/acoustic_signatures/complete_Noise_90 dBA_spectrogram_display.pdf}
	\caption{Spectrograms from internal and external sensors during \SI{90}{\deci\bel A} external noise}
	\label{fig:90_spectrogram}
\end{figure}

The spectrograms show the infiltration of the impulses from the noise, distinct in channel seven, manifest synchronously in the internal piezoelectric channels and match the impulses observed in recordings of a single insect. After applying the bandpass filtering from \SIrange{500}{6000}{\hertz}, the enveloping, and self-noise normalization, the impulses generated by the external noise signals are indistinguishable from those produced by insects.

Analyzing the spectra of the signals reveals that a more detailed spectral analysis can provide a means to differentiate between insect generated impulses and those from external noise. Figure \ref{fig:spectral_comparison} compares the spectra measured by a piezoelectric sensor channel zero during \SI{90}{\deci\bel A} external noise with \textit{T. confusum} in flour and \textit{C. maculatus} in oatmeal. 

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/external_noise/spectra_comparison.pdf}
	\caption{Comparison of insect, noise, and reference spectra}
	\label{fig:spectral_comparison}
\end{figure}

Although the noise signal permeates the internal sensor, especially at frequencies below \SI{1500}{\hertz}, the insect generated impulses have a higher spectral power above that frequency. This demonstrates that this lower frequency bound can be effectively utilized for detection purposes even in the presence of strong ambient noise.

\subsection{Optimizing Frequency Range to Limit External Noise Influence}

The NSPA algorithm was parametrized in order to find the lower and upper frequency limits that would enable the optimal detection of insect signals while minimizing the impact of external noise. Twenty samples of the insect signals representative of the median strength for each respective insect-material pairing combination were selected for analysis. The frequency bands were systematically varied and the respective NSPA values calculated and compared to the NSPA values of the external noise at \SI{90}{\deci\bel A} at the same range.

Although the NSPA difference significantly increases when the low range is above \SI{1500}{\hertz}, the high frequency bound plays a less critical role maximizing at around \SI{4000}{\hertz}. Focusing on the low frequency range, Figure \ref{fig:nspa_low_frequency} shows the NSPA difference between the insect and noise signals as the low frequency bound is varied while the high frequency bound is fixed at \SI{6000}{\hertz}. The optimal low frequency bound was determined to be \SI{1565}{\hertz} which was where the maximal NSPA difference converged to within \SI{5}{\percent}.
